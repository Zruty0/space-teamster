# The Estella System

*A Surveyor's Guide for New Arrivals (and Anyone Else Who Will Sit Still For It)*

---

## On the Star

The star at the center of the Estella system has, like most stars, a long and dignified catalogue index that nobody uses. Officially, it is HD-something-or-other. In practice, everyone calls it Estella, and has done since the first crewed Highliner committed to the system several generations ago.

The naming was not, as new arrivals sometimes assume, a tribute to the goddess of starlight or some other classical figure. **Estella was the wife of Captain Kasimir Volker**, master of that first Highliner, and the naming was — depending on whose account you believe — a romantic gesture, a public apology, an attempt to repair a faltering marriage, or simply the most expensive piece of jewelry a man has ever attempted to give a woman.

The marriage did not survive the return trip. The name did. There is a sermon in there somewhere, but most Teamsters skip it.

Estella herself is a yellow star of unremarkable size and temperature, similar enough to humanity's home star that early surveyors nearly skipped past on the assumption that anything this ordinary couldn't possibly be interesting. They were correct in the technical sense and wrong in every other.

> **Note for the writer:** Captain Volker is a placeholder name and may be replaced or elaborated freely. The marriage story is the only fixed lore element — it is the reason the system has the name it does, and crops up periodically in barroom anecdotes, sentimental ballads, and at least one award-winning opera that everyone agrees is not very good.

## The System at a Glance

Estella has fourteen bodies of significant size, a diffuse asteroid belt, two stellar-orbit stations of considerable industrial importance, and several thousand smaller rocks of which the catalogue still occasionally adds new entries. Approximately two hundred sites are known to be inhabited, occupied, garrisoned, or otherwise actively used. The rest are inhabited only by surprise.

The system is divided, by colonist convention rather than astronomical decree, into **five regions**. These are not formal political entities. They are the names working people use when they tell other working people where they have just been or where they are unfortunately going next.

| Region | What's there | What it feels like |
|---|---|---|
| **The Hearth** | Inner planets, civilization, atmosphere, traffic | Home — for those who have one |
| **The Camps** | Mid-system industrial planets | A worksite. Not a home. |
| **The Belt** | Asteroids, dwarf planets, the system's economic spine | Frontier — but the *populated* frontier |
| **The Wells** | Three gas giants and their many moons | Outer frontier, deep gravity, weird chemistry |
| **The Reach** | Cold ice dwarfs, comet swarms, lost outposts | The end of the line, more or less literally |

The system's center of human gravity is **the Caravanserai**, a massive industrial-civic asteroid in the Belt. It is the primary port of call for arriving and departing **Highliners** — the great interstellar vessels that connect Estella to the rest of human space. Without Highliners there is no trade with elsewhere. Without trade with elsewhere there is no fuel for engines. Without fuel for engines there are no Teamsters. Without Teamsters there is, very quickly, no economy.

This brief chain of dependency is not a coincidence.

## Origins

The first Highliner to commit to Estella found, broadly speaking, what surveys had promised: a yellow star, four rocky inner planets, three gas giants, a respectable Belt, and a cold dark Reach beyond. The star was charted, the bodies catalogued, the Belt prospected, and a small initial colony established at a particularly large industrial asteroid that has since grown into the Caravanserai.

Subsequent Highliners arrived at irregular intervals — sometimes years apart, sometimes decades — each bringing further colonists, further factions, further machinery, and (most importantly) further fuel. Each Highliner triggered an **Era**: a period of intense activity, trade, immigration, faction politics, and barroom violence, lasting from the Highliner's arrival until its departure approximately a month later. Between Eras, the system reverts to its quieter resident rhythm — the **Interim** — during which residents work, rest, plot, breed, and occasionally remember what they came here for.

The cumulative result of this irregular but inexorable accretion is the Estella System as it is today: **modestly populated, economically functional, politically complicated, and almost entirely dependent on a single fuel-and-engine monopoly known as the Teamsters' Guild.**

> **Writer's note:** The number of Highliners that have arrived to date, the names of the most historically significant ones, the cultural waves they brought, and the factions they seeded are all available canvas. Suggested anchor: at least one Highliner per major faction the writer wants to seed.

## Naming Conventions

A note on how things are named, since it will save the reader from confusion later.

There are three layers of names in this system, corresponding to three eras of who was doing the naming.

**Surveyors' catalogue** — Bodies have scientific designations. The star is *Estella*. Planets and major dwarfs are numbered radially outward in Roman numerals: *Estella I, II, III…* through *Estella XIV*. Moons get a lowercase letter suffix: *Estella IIIa, IIIb*, and so on.

Asteroids and other no-SOI bodies receive numeric catalogue entries of the form **ES-X-NNNN**, where the middle letter denotes spectral / compositional class:

| Class | Composition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **C** | carbonaceous — dark, primitive, volatile-bearing, the most common type | most cluster asteroids; the Caravanserai |
| **S** | silicaceous (stony) — rocky, structurally stable | observatories, science sites |
| **M** | metallic — iron-nickel, dense, valuable, ore-rich | the Still, foundry asteroids, industrial refineries |
| **I** | icy — high water/volatile content; comets, captured KBOs | the Reach comet swarm, outer captures |

Each class has its own sequential number, four digits, zero-padded: *ES-C-0001*, *ES-M-0001*, *ES-S-0001*, *ES-I-0001*, and so on.

POIs *on* a body take a dot suffix: *Estella IIIa.3* or *ES-C-0001.4*. The catalogue is exact, comprehensive, and used mostly by surveyors and bureaucrats.

**Colonist vernacular** — Working people don't talk like surveyors. Regions and the iconic places within them have ordinary names that ordinary people actually use. *The Hearth*, *The Caravanserai*, *The Still*. These names are working-class English — short, concrete, definite-article-prefixable, and immediate.

**Settler-given names** — As places become *lived in*, they accrue real names. Bars, neighborhoods, hostelries, ports, settlements, factory districts. Some are named after founders, some after the first dead person, some after cargo that once exploded there. The process is organic and the writer holds the brush.

**One useful diagnostic:** the *naming density* of a region tells you how settled it is. The Hearth has hundreds of names. The Reach has almost none.

# I. The Hearth

The Hearth is the inner ring of the Estella system: four planets and one moon, two of which are habitable, one of which is barely habitable in a particular and well-defended layer of its own atmosphere, and one of which is mostly used to throw rocks at the star.

This is the closest thing the system has to a *civilization*. Capital cities, government, regulation, fragile cargo, expensive everything. The traffic is dense, the customs are picky, the food is excellent, and the rent is criminal.

If you can afford to live in the Hearth, you generally do. If you can't, you go work the Camps or the Belt and dream of one day affording to come home.

## The Hearth's economy

Hearth is a **consumer of everything** — Camps machinery, Belt fuel, Wells volatiles, Reach exotica, Highliner imports. It is also a **producer of high-end output**: precision electronics, pharmaceuticals, luxury goods, finished medical, and cultural product (which is sometimes also literal product). And the Hearth is the source of the **stellar antimatter precursor** that the Teamsters' Guild refines into all the system's fuel — which is to say, the Hearth literally powers everything else, even when it doesn't pay attention.

Internal supply chains are dense and largely uninteresting to the outsider. The chains that *cross* into the Hearth from elsewhere are where most Teamster work originates.

# II. The Camps

The Camps are the mid-system industrial planets, where things get *made*. They are not, by general consensus, places where people *live* — they are places where people *work*, on shifts, on tours, on contracts, on assignment. The habitable surfaces are mostly sealed habs, dust-blown plains, weather-gated factory districts, and worker accommodations of the sort designed by the lowest-bidding contractor.

The Camps' population at any given time is overwhelmingly transient. Crews come for tours of months or years, accumulate hazard pay, and leave. A small permanent population maintains the infrastructure and quietly outlives the people who pay them.

The food is functional. The pay is decent. The dust gets in everything. The beer is excellent, because there's nothing else to do.

## The Camps' economy

This is where the system's *industrial backbone* lives. Foundries, shipyards, factories, agriculture (both bulk and specialty), high-vacuum precision manufacturing. The major cross-region corridor is **Belt → Camps → everywhere**: raw ore and Belt feedstocks enter the Camps, get processed into finished goods, and ship out across the system.

The Camps' planets (six bodies in three planetary systems) cover an unusually wide spread of atmospheres and surface conditions, making it the system's flight school: thin CO₂ atmospheres on one planet, thick cold weather-driven atmospheres on another, and an airless industrial planet for vacuum operations. A pilot who has worked the Camps can fly more or less anywhere.

# III. The Belt

The Belt is the diffuse ring of asteroids and small bodies that orbits the star between the Camps and the Wells. It is the system's nervous system: the place where everything *moves*.

The Hearth has the population, in the sense that the great majority of the system's residents live there and stay there. The Camps have the labor, in the sense that the system's industrial work happens there on shift contracts. **The Belt has the *traffic*** — Highliners, immigrants, traders, smugglers, prospectors, faction couriers, fortune-seekers, fugitives, and the entire moving cast of independent operators that the regulated Hearth does not welcome and the contract-bound Camps do not pay. If the Hearth is where the system *lives*, the Belt is where it *moves*.

The Belt is the home of the **Caravanserai**, the system's primary Highliner port; of **the Still**, the only fuel synthesis facility in Estella; and of two small dwarf planets, three major industrial asteroids, and approximately fifty smaller asteroids organized into five recognized clusters.

It is also home to the **Teamsters' Guild**, in the sense that the Guild is everywhere but is *run* from here.

## The Belt's economy

The Belt is the **economic spine of the system**. Every Highliner that enters Estella docks here. Every gram of fuel synthesized in the system is born here. Most contracts are posted here, accepted here, and (eventually) settled here.

The Belt's own native economy is mining, refining, and refueling — not glamorous, but central. It is also the home base for most independent operators: Teamsters, smugglers, merchants, salvage crews, and the kind of small-scale entrepreneurs who do not survive in the Hearth's regulatory climate.

## Cluster Mode

A note on geography. The Belt's small asteroids are organized into five **clusters** — tight groups of bodies that occupy roughly the same orbital region. Within a cluster, asteroids are close enough that travel between them is simple, low-Δv, and works at a different scale than interplanetary flight. Travel *between* clusters, however, is a real orbital transfer.

Three of the clusters share the Caravanserai's orbit and differ only by their phase angle. Two are in different orbits entirely.

| Cluster | Position | Gating | Flavor |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cluster 1 — The Near** | Co-orbital with the Caravanserai (close phase) | Open | First-contract rocks: small mines, prospectors, beginner work. *Contains the Caravanserai itself.* |
| **Cluster 2 — The Working** | Co-orbital, mid phase | Open | Active industrial mid-Belt |
| **Cluster 3 — The Outer Drift** | Co-orbital, far phase | Open | Sparser, more varied, longer transfers |
| **Cluster 4 — The Wreckage Field** | Different orbit (eccentric) | **Salvage license required** | Dense field, navigation hazard, salvage flavor |
| **Cluster 5 — The Quiet Side** | Different orbit (outer co-planar) | **Reputation-gated** | Smuggler dead-drops, rare and valuable, fewer jobs |

Each cluster contains roughly ten small asteroids, each one a single docking site. Catalogue IDs are a mix of ES-C-NNNN, ES-S-NNNN, and ES-M-NNNN depending on composition (most are C-class, with a scattering of S and M asteroids prized by miners). Names of individual cluster asteroids are writer territory.

# IV. The Wells

The Wells are the three gas giants of the Estella system and their attendant moons. The name comes from Teamster slang: gas giants are the *deepest gravity wells in the system*. Drop into the well, climb out of the well. Every trip into the Wells costs real Δv, which is to say, real money.

The gas giants themselves are not landable, in the sense that even attempting it would constitute the most expensive form of suicide currently available. They *can* be skimmed — the Guild and a handful of specialty operators do this for industrial gas — but the work is dangerous and the operators are few.

What matters in the Wells are the **moons**. The three giants between them host fourteen major moons of significant size and gameplay interest, plus a number of smaller captured asteroids and comets that orbit the giants but have no SOI of their own. These captured bodies — informally "the captives" — are docking-only sites, much like the Belt's small asteroids, except they orbit a gas giant instead of the star. They take catalogue IDs in the ES-C-NNNN, ES-I-NNNN, or occasionally ES-M-NNNN ranges depending on composition (the outer Wells lean icy).

A Teamster who has worked the Wells has, by definition, made multi-moon transfers, atmospheric entries on at least one moon (the system's only thick methane atmosphere is here), and probably a flyby or two of bodies they were definitely not supposed to be near.

## The Wells' economy

The Wells are the system's **major input producer**. Volatiles (water, ammonia, methane, hydrogen), exotic chemistry, rare elements, industrial gases, and a particular line of specialty ships designed for outer-system operations — most of these originate in the Wells and are exported to the Camps and the Hearth for further processing or consumption.

A great deal of the Wells' chains are *internal* to a single giant's system of moons. Multi-moon "tours" are a recognized mission type and the closest thing the system has to a "campaign" within a single contract.

# V. The Reach

The Reach is what lies beyond the gas giants. It is sparsely populated, cold, slow, and occupied mostly by people who have a specific reason to be that far from everything else: research, religion, exile, smuggling, retirement, or the slow accretion of personal fortune through long-haul logistics.

It is not, technically, the end of the system. The Estella catalogue has bodies past the Reach, but they are uninhabited rocks of catalogue interest only. The Reach is the *settled* outer limit.

## The Reach's economy

The Reach is, in net, an **import destination**. Most cargo into the Reach comes from elsewhere: supplies, food, parts, prisoners, exiles, scientists, religious pilgrims, rare equipment. A modest amount flows out: bulk ice, exotic isotopes, occasional research samples, occasional artifacts of dubious provenance.

There are no chained 3-stop loops in the Reach. The economic gameplay is **single-leg long-hauls**: a Teamster takes a contract from somewhere into the Reach, or from the Reach to somewhere else, and that single leg *is* the work.

A Reach contract is the highest-paying, lowest-volume, most prestigious work an independent Teamster can pick up. Many never bother. Those who do, do not forget.

# VI. Institutions

## The Teamsters' Guild

The Teamsters' Guild is the system's fuel-and-engine monopoly. Its formal name varies by Era and by who you ask; the vernacular is *the Teamsters*, *the Guild*, or — in unfriendly company — words less suitable for print.

The Guild is officially neutral. In practice, it is the single most powerful organization in Estella, by virtue of the simple fact that everything that *moves* in the system requires fuel, and all the fuel comes from the Guild. The Guild does not own ships. The Guild does not own planets. The Guild owns the *fuel*, the *engines*, and (in the manner of monopolies everywhere) the schedule.

### What the Guild sells
- Fuel (system-wide, every dispensary)
- Engines, drives, maneuvering, RCS
- Teamster certifications (skill certs are Guild-administered)
- Insurance and debt (the Guild is also the lender of last resort)

### What the Guild does not sell
Hulls, electronics, life support, cargo systems, weapons. These come from third-party outfitters — most prominently at the Caravanserai. The Guild thus has its hand on the *propulsion* of every ship in the system, but does not own the ship itself. A ship can be Guild-fueled and Guild-engined while flying for, say, a Hearth syndicate, a Reach religious order, or a Camps cooperative.

### The supply chain
The Guild's fuel is synthesized by a process which (depending on whom you ask, in what tone of voice) is either a marvel of human engineering or a closely guarded fraud. The technical version, broadly:
1. Skim ships harvest **stellar antimatter precursor** from the Hearth's near-star skim hubs (Skim Hub Alpha, Skim Hub Beta, Coronal Observation Post).
2. Skim ships transit precursor to the Belt and deliver to **the Still**.
3. The Still synthesizes precursor into stable fuel canisters.
4. The Still distributes fuel canisters to dispensaries throughout the system.

The chain is system-spanning, single-vendor, and proprietary. Disruption to any single stage is, in principle, a system-wide fuel crisis. In practice, the Guild has been operating long enough that it has redundancies the rest of the system does not see.

> **Writer's note:** The Guild's culture, factions within the Guild, the politics of Guild leadership, the relationship between the skim crews and the refiners, and the dirty laundry of the Guild's history are all open canvas. So is the question of how the Guild responds when other factions try to *break* the monopoly. (They have, periodically. None has succeeded. Yet.)

## Highliners

A **Highliner** is a great interstellar cargo vessel. It is slow, vast, expensive to build, and operated by a captain and crew who are usually their own faction in some sense.

Highliners arrive at irregular intervals — every few in-game months on average, but historically anywhere from weeks to years apart. Each Highliner stays in-system for roughly a month before departing for its next destination, which may be elsewhere in human space and may or may not ever return.

The arrival of a Highliner triggers an **Era**. During an Era:
- The Highliner docks at the Caravanserai (or, occasionally, parks in a near-orbit if its size or cargo profile demands)
- A wave of new contracts opens, biased toward the Highliner's origin and destination cargo
- The Highliner's faction(s) become temporarily prominent in system politics
- Premium "direct-to-Highliner" missions become available
- The Caravanserai's Foreign Quarter becomes Era-flavored — different NPCs, different rumors, different jobs

When the Highliner departs, its Era ends. The system reverts to the **Interim**: quieter, smaller mission volume, more local Belt and Camps work, less faction-flavored cargo.

Each Highliner is a named, distinct event with its own captain, origin system, destination system, faction associations, and cargo profile. **The writer authors the Highliners.**

> **Writer's note:** Suggested anchor: at minimum, a roster of named Highliners (5–10 to start) covering the major factional / cultural archetypes the writer wants to seed. Ideally each Highliner is interestingly different — a religious vessel, a mercenary contract carrier, a corporate freight monolith, a colony ship-of-pilgrims, a research consortium, a smuggler-in-good-standing. Eras can be replayed (the same Highliner returns) or unique (one-time arrivals), depending on what the narrative needs.

# VII. Notes for the Writer

This document is a *backbone*. It is intended to be sufficient to write against, but not so detailed that there is no canvas left.

The following are explicitly **writer territory**:
- All proper names not already locked. Catalogue IDs (`Estella IIIa`, `ES-C-0001`, `ES-M-0001`) are canonical. Vernacular names (*The Caravanserai*, *The Still*, *Olympos*) are locked. Everything else — every settlement, district, bar, faction subdivision, ship, character, Highliner, ritual, accent, and historical event — is yours.
- The cultural and biome distinction between Gaia (Estella III) and Dahai (Estella IV). Make them rivals, kin, complements, or whatever serves.
- The history and reception of the star's name (was the marriage public? was Estella herself ever invited? did she refuse? did she eventually visit?).
- The faction roster beyond the Teamsters' Guild.
- The Highliner roster, history, and politics.
- The deeper stories of the abandoned-but-active sites (Estella V.6, Estella XIV.4, the deepest dock, and several others throughout).
- The actual industrial and political workings of ES-C-0002 the casino asteroid, and of the Foreign Quarter at the Caravanserai.
- The cultural waves brought by historic Highliners.
- Whatever, if anything, is buried in or behind the Reach.

The following are **locked** (changing them disrupts the geography or the gameplay):
- The five regions and their names (Hearth, Camps, Belt, Wells, Reach).
- The catalogue scheme and the radial ordering of bodies.
- The list of bodies and their basic types (atmospheric / airless / dwarf / asteroid / gas giant / etc.).
- The economic role of each region.
- The Teamsters' Guild as system-wide fuel monopoly.
- The Highliner Era / Interim rhythm.
- The Caravanserai as home base, the Still as fuel refinery.

If something here is in your way, push back. Most of it is in the document because it serves a gameplay constraint we can't easily change, but a few of these can be loosened with a conversation.
